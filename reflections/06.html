Reflection 06:

Exploring the Philosophical Foundations of Education

Studying the different educational philosophies such as Essentialism, Progressivism, Perennialism, Existentialism, Behaviorism, Linguistic Philosophy and Constructivism has provided me with good insight on how I would like to teach.

Teaching nurturing is a principle held in place by essentialism, which seeks to focus on the basics and beginning, I thought this was the most efficient way to prepare the learners for basic knowledge and skills. However, I also know that no matter how much content is good for students, the content also needs to be entertaining for learners and make them interested and curious. In my case, I want to combine cognitive inputs and creativity while being a teacher so that my students understand the material is necessary but fun too.

Out of all the instructional ideologies, I simply embrace progressivism because of the emphasis on learner-centered education via use of critical thinking abilities. This I find how the approach helps the students to be ready to face real life situations.T Thinking about it more, I realize that Iâ€™d like to build a learning environment into which students will be eager to learn new things, make inquiries, and apply this knowledge to their own lives. It is my belief: This philosophy compels me to offer more engaging lessons instead of the ordinary approach we sometimes use in the classroom.

Perennialism are based on eternal knowledge and classic works. While I acknowledge the validity of confronting students with these timeless ideas, at times I wonder how topical they could be in such rapidly changing times. As a teacher, I am going to need to reconcile tradition with modern thinking and find ways in which those timeless teachings could possibly make some difference in my students' lives.

Existentialism emphasizes choice, thereby giving individual meaning to life. It made me change my consideration of importance by allowing students to voice their opinions when I make decisions in the classroom. According to students, it is important that they feel empowered and be able to explore their identities; however, as the teacher, I have to balance personal freedom with the need for structure in the classroom.

Behaviorism is a type that focuses on observable behaviors and reinforcement. Although highly effective for classroom management, I am afraid that this approach may limit students' creativity and intrinsic motivation. I want to motivate my future students in ways that will make them curious and love learning instead of waiting for rewards or fear of punishment.

Linguistic Philosophy made my eyes open to the power of language in education. When I speak in certain ways, it will influence how students understand and get involved with such ideas. Knowing that as a teacher, I have to pay attention to the language I employ in how it helps make my students learn through it. Clear communication makes students confident about what they think and say.

Lastly, Constructivism encourages active learning where children find things out for themselves through experience. I find this approach very attractive because it emphasizes a type of personal discovery. Reflecting on this, I realize how crucial it is to establish such a setting in the classroom, one where students feel empowered to explore, make mistakes, and construct knowledge in a meaningful way.

The philosophies of teaching and learning are more than the mere attempt at transferring knowledge. The philosophies create a learning environment, thus aiding in the progression of students in different ways. I look forward to taking the best from each philosophy in an attempt to form my own unique blend that leads me in the direction of what I want to do for my future students.
